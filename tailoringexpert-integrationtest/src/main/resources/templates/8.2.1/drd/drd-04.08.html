DRD-04.08 - Failure Mode, Effect & Criticality Analysis (FMECA)
Reference: MIL-HDBK-338 Electronic Reliability Design Handbook
ECSS-Q-ST-30-02C Space product assurance - Failure Modes, effects (and criticality) analysis (FMEA/FMECA)
Purpose: The analysis shall be used to:
  • identify single point failures,
  • identify critical items,
  • ensure that adequate telemetry and command capabilities are available to detect and control failures,
  • identify design incompatibilities and inadequacies,
  • verify, as far as possible, that all redundant elements and all critical and major failure modes can be fully tested at integrated system and subsystem level,
  • clarify the effects of transients,
  • identify monitoring and test capabilities in orbit,
  • identify back-up operation modes and procedures.
The FMECA results shall be used to update the project/system requirements and to drive its design along the project life cycle.
Content: The FMECA can be initiated at any level of integration depending on the information available and the requirements of the specific programme/project.
Failure modes of each block in the Functional Block Diagram or Functional Tree shall be analysed, indicating the effect of item failure on the performance of the next higher assembly level. A numerical reference for all items in the Functional Block Diagram or Functional Tree must be provided by using a standard coding system.
The FMECA Report shall include all information necessary for understanding and reviewing the analysis:
  • identification of configuration being analysed,
  • identification of applicable functional and design description,
  • identification of equipment, subsystem, system mission phase and operational phase under consideration,
  • identification of item under consideration, its function, the assumed failure mode and the effect on other equipment/subsystems,
  • appropriate design information (e.g. assembly drawings, circuit diagrams, etc. as far as necessary for understanding),
  • Functional Diagram or Functional Tree,
  • Reliability Block Diagram,
  • identification of criticality categories,
  • recommendations to improve the design.
The analysis shall be supported by lower level FMECA. All end effects of a lower level FMECA shall become failure modes of the higher level FMECA (see figure Cause and effects of failure modes at different level FMECAs). When any design/process changes are made, the FMECA will be updated and the effects of new failure modes introduced by the changes will be carefully assessed.
All identified SPFs shall be listed in a Single Point Failure List.
Format: The FMECA shall be presented on forms according to ECSS-Q-ST-30-02C or equivalent in-house forms. The requirements of ECSS-Q-ST-30-02C may be tailored by the customer to suit specific programmes. The FMECA worksheet shall contain the data elements identified in the FMEA. SW reaction to HW failures are addressed by the Hardware Software Interaction Analysis (HASIA), if required.
FMECA Worksheet
  1. The documentation of the FMEA/FMECA shall be accomplished by completing the columns of the customer approved worksheet.
    An example of FMEA/FMECA worksheet is shown in the figure. The use of this worksheet is not mandatory but recommended.
  2. Each FMEA/FMECA worksheet shall include in the header basic information including
    • Identity of the product (hardware or function)
    • Identity of corresponding equipment, subsystem and system (as applicable).
  3. The FMEA/FMECA worksheet shall provide the following data elements:
    1. Identification number (for traceability purposes)
    2. Item/block
      1. the name of the item or function being analysed, and
      2. the block of the reliability block diagram that is applicable to the analysis entry.
    3. Function
      concise statement of the function performed by the item.
    4. Failure mode
      identification and description of all potential failure modes of the item or function under analysis.

      end effects of lower level FMECA are failure modes of the higher level FMECA

    5. Failure cause
      identification and description of the most probable causes associated with the assumed failure mode.

      failure modes of lower level FMECA are failure causes of the higher level FMECA.

      The failure cause are generally not identified when components are analysed (equipment level FMECA).

    6. Mission phase/Operational mode concise statement of the mission phase and operational mode in which the failure is assumed to occur.

      These elements can be addressed in the header of the worksheet. Although all of the different mission phases or operational modes are taken into account, the record of results is limited to the phase or mode in which the worst failure effects occur.

    7. Failure effects
      Identification of the consequences of each assumed failure mode at local effects and end effects levels.

      Local effects concentrate specifically on the impact of the failure mode on the operation, function, or status of the item identified in the second column of the worksheet. The local effects are recorded when different from the failure modes.
      The purpose of defining local effects is to provide a basis for evaluating compensating provisions and for recommending corrective actions.

      End effects define the effect that the analysed failure mode has on the operation, function, or status of the product under investigation and its interfaces, such that it allows integration into the next higher level FMECA.

    8. Severity
      classification category assigned to each failure mode according to the worst potential end effect of the failure
    9. Failure detection method - Observable symptoms
      Describe the existing methods by which the effects can be detected.

      The failure detection means include telemetry (exact label), visual or audible warning devices, sensing instrumentation, other unique indications (e.g. the failure effect itself), or none.

    10. Compensating provisions
      Identify the existing compensating provisions, such as design provisions or operator actions, which circumvent or
      mitigate the effect of the failure. Describe the existing preventive or compensatory provisions to prevent the failure mode, to reduce its effects, or to reduce its probability of occurrence.

      Compensating provisions are considered design provisions when they feature a design that nullifies the effects of a malfunction or failure, control, or deactivate product items to halt generation or propagation of failure effects, or activate backup or standby items. Design compensating provisions include:

      • redundant items or alternative modes of operation that allow continued and safe operation, and
      • safety or relief devices which allow effective operation or limit the failure effects.

        Compensating provisions are considered operator actions when the operator circumvents or mitigates the effect of the postulated failure mode.

    11. Severity number
      Identify the severity of a failure effect by assigning a severity number (SN).
    12. Failure mode probability
      assessment of the probability of occurrence of the assumed failure mode and the relevant probability number (PN)
    13. Criticality number
      Enter the criticality number (CN) by multiplying  SN x PN
    14. Recommendations and remarks
      Describe recommended preventive or compensatory provisions to eliminate the failure mode, to reduce its effects, to reduce its probability of occurrence, or to improve its detectability, as well as any additional information being useful.
